Monique Louis

*This essay was originally submitted in November 2021 and recalls facts/statistics from that year.

Unsportsmanlike Culture: Roughing the Players

The list of competitors with the most Super Bowl wins starts with Tom Brady, who has more rings than any of the NFL’s 32 teams. He hoisted the Lombardi trophy in six out of nine Super Bowl appearances with the New England Patriots and one more earlier this year in his first season playing for the Tampa Bay Buccaneers. His multiple passing records and historic career as a quarterback can be attributed to his undisputed greatness, but also his longevity. Brady, 44, is the oldest player in the league; no other current NFL athlete is above 40. He claims he’ll make it to 50, surpassing legendary kicker Adam Vinatieri, who retired at 48. Brady and Vinatieri, however, are outliers. The average retirement age in the NFL is 27.6 years, with many players retiring young due to injuries. So, what makes Brady an exception?

Tom Brady is a quarterback, the most protected position in football, both by the game’s design and its rules. The basic function of the offensive line is to block defenders from getting to the quarterback, allowing him enough time to pass the ball. Unless the quarterback is also a runner, which Brady tends not to be (he averages 1.6 yards per carry, about one-third as much as this generation’s mobile quarterbacks), he won’t get hit unless he holds onto the ball for too long and the attacking defenders reach him.

Meanwhile, in recent years, the NFL has been increasing protections for players, with particular attention to quarterbacks. Between 1993 and 2017, there have been six major rule changes to protect quarterbacks, including ones prohibiting defenders from hitting them or landing on them with full force while or after they throw a pass, allowing the quarterback to throw the ball out of bounds (past the line of scrimmage) to avoid a sack, and banning blows below the waist. The NFL has also increased penalties for violating existing rules, such as personal fouls, and has outlawed certain tackles, like horse-collar tackles and helmet-to-helmet hits. Some fans, commentators, and players have criticized these changes—among them, Tom Brady. In an interview with the podcast Let’s Go! Brady noted that referees are calling penalties on violent plays that would not have been called 10 or 15 years ago. He reflected, “I’d say the game is a little softer than it used to be. I think the defensive players are more defensive when they go in to tackle [out of fear of being penalized].” At the same time,  he admitted that the penalties on hard hits are “probably adding to this element of quarterbacks [operating] outside the pocket and taking more chances… than they did in the past.”

Brady’s first statement reflects a belief widely held among the sport’s fans: that the new rules have made the game “soft” and thus less enjoyable. But the truth lies in his latter statement—those very same rules have made the NFL more fun. Mobile quarterbacks have become the face of the league, such as 2019 MVP Lamar Jackson and 2020 Super Bowl Champion Patrick Mahomes, who have added so much flair and introduced new possibilities to the game. The NFL has moved past a violent era in which dangerous hits went unpunished, paving the way for a more fun, innovative way for quarterbacks and their teammates to win games.

The new rules have ushered in a “golden age” for quarterbacks, who have been breaking records at unprecedented rates in the NFL, which is good: audiences should want to watch an exciting game, not their team’s quarterback being carted off the field with an injury that could affect him for the rest of his life. Limiting the worst aspects of football does not reduce the quality of the game; toughness is required to play football, not violence. Players should feel discouraged from harming their opponents, and they should follow the rules that enforce safety. Football is a rough sport by nature—it can’t go “soft.” Some fans, of course, appreciate the long balls, touchdowns, and interceptions. Others prefer hard hits and big tackles. But player safety shouldn’t be determined by fans’ preferences.

The glorification of hard hits reflects a dangerous mindset that hurts not just professional athletes but also collegiate and high school athletes. For many years, the NFL lied about the frequency and gravity of head trauma and concussions. In the face of anecdotal evidence, they denied the existence of Chronic Traumatic Encephalopathy (CTE)—a brain disease caused by recurrent head trauma, generating a protein in the central nervous system that kills brain cells—for two decades until research at Boston University revealed the extent of the problem. Memory loss, disorientation, paranoia, violent thoughts, anxiety, depression, and suicidal thoughts are just a few of the laundry list of symptoms characteristic of CTE. Because CTE has symptoms similar to cases of dementia and Parkinson's, it is extremely difficult—practically impossible—to diagnose. The only way to definitively know whether an athlete had CTE is through an autopsy after their death. In 2017, Boston University professor Dr. Ann McKee performed over 100 autopsies on former NFL athletes and found that all but one had signs of CTE. They found that every year of play increases an athlete’s chances of contracting CTE by 30%, a risk that doubled after only 2.6 years of play. Recent studies also show that CTE may begin to develop in athletes as young as 17. Many NFL athletes strongly believe they struggle with the disease, and it is highly likely they are right.

After public pressure on Capitol Hill, the NFL settled over one billion dollars worth of concussion lawsuits with some of its former players. Since then, it has begun making significant contributions to research on head trauma and helmet technology. Still, that hasn’t reduced the number of players who suffer from severe injuries in addition to concussions. Many players end up disabled, with permanent joint and muscle damage. The NFL’s early retirement rate is no coincidence; many athletes are forced to retire after they are unable to sufficiently recover from an injury.

To its credit, the NFL has completely changed its rhetoric on the injuries and head trauma that many of its athletes endure. Still, the NFL needs to do more to protect other players in positions that are more susceptible to head injuries, such as running backs, linebackers, and linemen. Even quarterbacks aren’t always protected. As soon as they become a runner, they lose the protections they originally had in the pocket (behind their line of defense). To further protect quarterbacks and other running players, the NFL should encourage clean tackles by banning all low and hard hits. Adding stiffer penalties seems to have helped. Defensive players have been hitting quarterbacks less to avoid attracting a 15-yard penalty. This allows America’s favorite quarterbacks (like Brady!) to play longer, which creates new opportunities for broken records and unforgettable plays.

The thrill of Sunday entertainment should not outweigh the players’ health and well-being.
