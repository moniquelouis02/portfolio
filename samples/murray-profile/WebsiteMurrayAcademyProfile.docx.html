Murray Language Academy Proves Value of Language Learning Amid Federal Rollbacks

By Monique Louis

When parents walk the halls at Murray Language Academy, math and English lessons echo from the first-floor classrooms. Climb the stairs to the second floor, and the sounds shift — instructions are spoken in Spanish, French, or Mandarin.

“I walked past the French class, and kindergarteners are speaking and recognizing words and talking back to the teacher (in French),” said Andrew Borders Jr., a parent of a sixth grader at the school. “It’s mindblowing.”

Murray Language Academy is kindergarten through eighth grade and offers instruction in three languages. Each student leaves Murray having learned either Spanish, French or Mandarin, which they choose when they enroll at the school. Murray is part of the Chicago Public School (CPS) system.

On March 31, President Donald Trump signed an executive order designating English as the U.S.’s official language. This act was one of several efforts to hinder world language education, particularly to English language learners, according to EducationWeek, an education-centered nonprofit newspaper. The organization reported that the Trump administration gutted the English language learning sect of the Department of Education and rescinded funding to English learning programs across the nation.

At a time when multi-lingual education is being targeted and discouraged at the highest level, Murray’s — and CPS’s — commitment to multilingual education offers a model for how language learning can build not just academic skills, but empathy and cultural understanding.

Melissa Korpalski, who has taught French at Murray for 13 years, estimates that only one third of her students are deeply passionate about learning a foreign language. But she posits that students develop other valuable and important skills — and create meaningful opportunities — from their multilingual education.

“It reinforces concepts in English and helps them become better writers and speakers in English," she started, “but it also instills a sense of empathy in students.”

Korpalski’s instincts about how multilingual education benefits her students are backed by a 2018 study published in the Foreign Language Annals, which shows that learning multiple languages at a young age sharpens children’s problem-solving skills. Learning two languages at once does not confuse a child; rather, it engages them further, enhancing their ability to ignore irrelevant information and can even reinforce their understanding of English grammar.

Korpalski also highlighted that being multilingual “improves career possibilities.” She pointed to herself as an example as someone who is employed because of her ability to speak and teach in two languages.

Murray’s Mandarin teacher, Maggie Ma, made this observation as well, saying, “I am a beneficiary of being bilingual.” Ma was born in China, and was able to move and teach in the United States because of her bilingual education.

While the increased cognitive abilities in children is a strong benefit of bilingual learning, Korpalski’s emphasis on building empathy in her students is a larger focus at Murray Academy.

Ma, a Chinese immigrant, has been teaching at Murray for three years. She sees herself as “a bridge between two languages and cultures.”

This vision of fostering cross-cultural understanding reflects the school's broader mission.

“Building empathetic people is exposing them to different types of people,” said principal Harrison Baker, who joined Murray three years ago. “If we can start identifying the similarities between us, that allows individuals to become more empathetic…. That foundation grows with them.”

Research supports Baker’s observations: the aforementioned study also found that multilingual students have a heightened cultural understanding, an increased empathy and cultural awareness, and reduced xenophobia through exposure to other cultures.

“They are very curious about other countries, other cultures,” Korpalski said. She explained that learning another language requires vulnerability. And through struggle and misunderstanding, students grow, thus deepening their “sense of empathy for others who are (also) experiencing that.”

CPS has three models of language learning programs: Dual Language Education (DLE), Foreign Language Exploratory Program (FLEX) and Foreign Language in Elementary Schools (FLES). The latter two programs pertain to CPS’s World Language Program.

CPS has 45 DLE schools, 234 FLEX schools and just four FLES schools. Murray is one of the four FLES programs in the city.

The distinction between the FLEX and FLES curriculums is the number of minutes dedicated to language learning. In the FLEX programs, students receive 120 minutes or fewer of foreign language instruction per week, whereas the FLES program mandates that students receive more than 120 minutes of foreign language instruction each week.

While students at Murray do not see their language instructors every day, they receive a more comprehensive foreign language education due to their increased presence in their language classrooms.

More importantly, language instruction begins at a much younger age compared to non-language academies, in Illinois and across the country. A 2017 report by the Commission on Language Learning revealed that in 2008, only 25% of U.S. public elementary schools offered a foreign language.

At Murray, students begin their language education in kindergarten.

Students, parents, and teachers all commented on the importance of learning a language young.

“They are sponges; they soak it all up,” said Korpalski.

In 2018, MIT scientists found that a person can only develop a proficiency similar to that of a native speaker if they begin their language studies before the age of 10. Meanwhile, most students in the U.S. begin their language studies in the 6th grade, after they’ve turned 11.

Parents take many factors into consideration when deciding where to send their children to school. And while Murray’s multilingual program is attractive, it has also fostered a culture that families love.

Lazarus Daniels, whose daughter attends Murray, loves that both the principal and assistant principal are Black. According to the Illinois Report Card, the school has 97.6% minority enrollment and is predominantly Black, so representation among the adults in the building is important to him, as well as Principal Baker.

“It is extremely important to have people in this building who look like our students (and) who understand our students culturally,” said Baker, who has been intentional about curating a positive, welcoming culture over his first three years at Murray.

Daniels’ 6-year-old daughter, Lyla, is taking French at Murray. She was recently awarded for earning all A’s and B’s. She said that the award, alongside the support of her teachers, makes her feel smart.

Another parent of an honor roll student, Andrew Borders Jr., said Murray encourages its students to strive for excellence. “Kids here… aren’t happy with B’s and C’s,” he said.

His daughter Drew is one of them. She has a B in math she is hoping will turn into an A by the end of the semester. If her math grade flips, her report card will show only A’s.  

The Borderses elected for their daughters to take Spanish when they enrolled them as kindergarteners. Drew believes that starting her Spanish education at a young age makes the language come more naturally to her.

“I like Spanish a lot. I’ve been taking it my whole life,” she remarked.

Although Drew enjoys the language, her favorite subject is math. Last summer, she attended a week-long STEM program at Purdue. Her father credits Murray with helping set his daughter up for success.

“When they leave Murray, they’ll be ready for the next step,” Borders Jr. said.

He and Daniels also cited career opportunities as a reason for enrolling their daughters in Murray's multilingual program.

Daniels called Murray “100% goal driven,” and said its teachers help students “polish” and reach their goals.

To principal Baker, parent involvement is paramount to student success. It starts with his biweekly newsletters, but his effort to include parents in education at Murray has expanded into increased programming as well, like the ‘Men of Murray’ initiative, which brings fathers and male role models into the building to serve as mentors and morning greeters, supplementing the school's existing before- and after-school programs that involve some parents.

He also introduced several reading programs, such as reading circles and nooks, which parents and kids enjoy.

Borders Jr. lauded Baker for creating a culture that he says the entire school community has embraced. Quality education for the students remains at the center of Baker's leadership, and Murray recently won a CPS award for academic growth.

Murray's popularity has created challenges. Approximately 40% of Murray’s students are from the neighborhood, and the remaining 60% enter the school through a lottery system. Because of this lottery system, enforced through CPS, Murray has had to turn away some immigrant families seeking multilingual education for their children — families who might benefit from the program amid Trump's cutbacks to English-learning programs — according to a social worker at the school.

Despite the Trump administration's threats to multilingual education, CPS is expanding its dual language and world language programs, adding them to six more schools next year.
