The Real Cost of COVID in Classrooms Isn't Low Test Scores

In March of 2020, classrooms around the country moved into the home. Desks turned into dining room tables. A teacher’s warm, demanding presence was rendered virtual in a world where they lost their authority. Education has been trying to find its footing since.

“So few kids were engaged,” recalled 7th and 8th grade math teacher Luke Albrecht, “they were obviously not paying attention, they were obviously not doing the work.”

The students who were engaged oftentimes had support systems at home who could keep them accountable.

One parent sat by their child every day during online learning, and witnessed how disengaged most of her son’s classroom was.”There were kids on the couch, asleep… (one time a student) got on the bed and started jumping,” said Kimberly Moore, whose son was in Kindergarten at the time. She said that her presence made a “big difference” in her son’s commitment to learning. He is currently a grade level ahead in math.

But most CPS parents did not have the time to be constantly present in their child’s education.

So, many kids fell behind.

The Illinois State Board of Education released its standardized assessment results on Oct. 15. There was a 10 point bump in CPS English and math proficiency (from 29.1% to 42% in English proficiency and from 18.4% to 27% in math proficiency, from 2024 to 2025). This notable increase is the result of the state lowering the cut scores—the target proficiency benchmarks. And even with these lowered standards, fewer than half of students are matching proficiency levels. In other words, students are still struggling, even with lowered expectations.

The state assessment scores were lowest in 2022 (20% English proficiency and 15% math proficiency), the second year testing after the pandemic. But scores were still low pre-COVID (27% English proficiency and 23% math proficiency pre-2020).

But educators across Hyde Park aren’t so worried about the low scores. Several teachers don’t believe the state assessment is an effective tool to get students where they need to be, and don’t believe the curriculum is conducive to scoring well on the test.

Furthermore, Shoesmith Elementary principal Kai Murray fears that lowering the cut scores only makes things more difficult for teachers and will harm students on their path to college and career readiness. “(We need to) decide on a benchmark and try to get our students there,” she said.

Many teachers believe the pandemic only exacerbated existing challenges in CPS—scores were already low before 2020. And the shifting standards in state testing only make things more difficult.

In response, teachers do not worry themselves with the state assessment scores so long as students continue to improve, which they are. CPS has recorded steady increases in English and math proficiency since 2022.

Rather than strictly focusing on achieving higher scores, teachers are grappling with COVID’s more pressing effects on their classrooms.  

Principal Murray said that attention-seeking behavior has been a notable fallout from the isolation of the pandemic. “We need to make sure that the social-emotional foundation is set,” said Murray, “then we can layer on the academics. Then we can layer on the rigor.”

Hala Abdulkarim, a 6th grade English teacher at Murray Language Academy, has had to incorporate teaching social skills to her students into her classroom every day. “I see their need to socialize,” she said. She teaches emotional recognition and regulation, something she said she didn’t have to teach before the pandemic.

Albrecht recognizes the importance of community in his classroom. He understands that his students need to feel supported before he can dive into math. Once a week, students write how they have felt that week on a Post-it and put it up on a board. It allows him to understand what they are going through and identify if they need additional support.

Over the doorframe to his classroom, a poster reads: Learn. Get Each Other’s Backs. Say Yes.

Photo courtesy of Luke Albrecht

“If you try to say yes to what we’re doing, then I’ll have your back, then you'll have others' backs,” Albrecht tells his students, “and then we'll get stuff done.”
