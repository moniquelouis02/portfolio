What Defines Us

Anthony Spaulding always knew he was special.

Even after he was sentenced to life in prison, without parole, at just 16 years old. He knew that one day, he would walk a free man.

After spending 27 years incarcerated at Stateville Correctional Facility in Crest Hill, Illinois, Anthony Spaulding works as a barber, a motivational speaker, a poet and a playwright, among several other ventures.

“He’s a go-getter,” said Kevin Galvez, who works at the shop with Spaulding, “he takes advantage of every opportunity that comes his way.”

After his release in 2022, Spaulding hit the ground running. He immediately enrolled in a barbering program and became certified to cut hair. But his previously incarcerated status only granted him a temporary six-month certification.

Again, Spaulding did not waver.

“We had to help him understand that now, everything is a process,” said his mentor Charles Hardwick, who aided Spaulding in his return to society. Hardwick, now the director of the Fresh Start program, helped Spaulding find a job so that he could re-apply for permanent certification.

Spaulding quickly found a job at a barbershop on State Street. Three years later, he opened his own shop—Anthony’s Barbershop—in Lincoln Park.

Spaulding poses at his barber shop in between clients

When Spaulding cuts hair, he is quick yet precise. Patient with the hair and detail oriented. Each haircut takes only 30 minutes. As he answered questions about his childhood, his eyes were trained on the fade he was blending. His fingers could switch guards in an instant.

Every once in a while, he would pause and look up with a smile.

“My father used to give us these messed up haircuts,” Spaulding reminisced, laughing.

It’s what inspired him to pick up the shears. He practiced on his two brothers, then his schoolmates, then the men in his neighborhood. He grew up in Roseland in the South Side of Chicago.

Though Spaulding’s father did not properly teach him how to cut hair, he taught him just about everything else.

They enjoyed fishing together and played several sports together, particularly baseball and basketball. His father had a military background, so he taught Spaulding how to fight. He picked up boxing and the martial arts, and really enjoyed it. Until it got him involved in gang activities.

In his community, getting wrapped up in gang culture “wasn’t if, it was when,” Spaulding said. Gang leaders recognized his fighting ability and asked him to become involved.

He was twelve years old when he first picked up a gun. He was also a straight A student.

“From 12 to 16, I was leading a double life,” Spaulding explained, “I was a star student and a star athlete.” But he still described himself as feeling constantly angry. And at 16, he was caught and charged with murder, a crime he expresses he deeply repents.

Despite being so young, Spaulding didn’t let this crime define his character. He was told that he couldn’t enroll in classes at the prison due to his life sentence. But he petitioned and took those classes anyway.

Spaulding turned to education in his time at Stateville. Every week, he studied under South Side artist and activist Margaret T.G. Burroughs, who came to the facility to teach creative writing and poetry. He wrote thirteen books — some finished in a week — of which three have been published. He took up cutting hair again. And he turned to spirituality and faith to guide him through it all.

In 2012, the Supreme Court ruled that juveniles cannot be sentenced to life without parole. In 2012, Spaulding applied for clemency.

He had done it before, three times, but this time the judges had to hear his case. He prepared, using Burroughs’ teachings of confidence and conviction, to present his case.

Anthony Spaulding only looks forward. Since leaving Stateville, he has built a life defined by momentum.

He owns a business, he speaks to young kids about his life, he writes poetry and plays and books. Each day starts at 5 a.m. and stretches into the late evening.

“Everything he's done once he’s come home, he's succeeded at it” said Hardwick.

And still, the most admirable thing about Spaulding is his illuminating positivity.

“He is never not smiling,” said his co-worker Galvez. His clients arrive excited to come speak with him.

Spaulding knows he is loved, appreciated, and accomplished. Yet he is humble and future-oriented.

Even with his mountain of accomplishments — which he has achieved in only three years since his release — Spaulding stays hungry. His next undertaking is producing a feature film. He is currently applying to film schools in Chicago.

Spaulding, at 46, has spent more time incarcerated than as a free man. But his life is defined by his determination, his persistence, his hard work, and his unwavering positivity.

“If you can dream it, you can make it happen,” he said. He's spent a lifetime proving it. That endless belief — even when locked away for life, even when the world told him no — that's what makes him special.
